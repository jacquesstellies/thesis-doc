\chapter{Literature Review}
\label{chap:literature_review}

The problem of fault tolerant control systems (FTCS) has gained signifcant attention in the literature in the last few decades, with a variety of methods proposed to handle different types of faults and scenarios. The problem is usually broken down in to two main subproblems: fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) and fault tolerant control (FTC) \cite{yinReviewRecentDevelopment2016}. FDD involves identifying the presence of a fault, diagnosing the type of fault, and estimating the severity of the fault. FTC involves designing reconfigurable control laws that can operate in the presence of the identified faults. Typically some switching logic is used to determine which control law to use based on the identified faults.

\section{Fault Tolerant Control}

Some common FTC methods are sliding mode control (SMC), backstepping control, model predictive control (MPC), and adaptive control \cite{yinReviewRecentDevelopment2016}. These methods have been applied to a variety of systems, including spacecraft attitude control systems (ACS) with reaction wheel faults.

In \cite{jiangAdaptiveBacksteppingFaulttolerant2010} a backstepping sliding-mode adaptive controller is designed to handle reaction wheel faults where a redundant actuator is available. unknown faults of the reaction wheels, external disturbances, and unknown inertia matrix of the spacecraft. It is assumed that the angular velocity Ω of the reaction wheels are within the saturation limit and the input control torque u(t) is unbounded.
